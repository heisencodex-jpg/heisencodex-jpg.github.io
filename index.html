I Wrote 46 Rules for a German Job-Visa Classifier. Only 14 Ever Fire.

And 98.57% of rows say "I don't know." Here's what 1,679 real job listings taught me about shipping honest data products.

Last week I published germany-visa-jobs, an Apify Actor that pulls listings from Germany's federal job board and stamps each one with a visa-sponsorship label: confirmed, likely, unlikely, or unknown. Pay-per-result. The README claimed "~93% unknown" because that's what I eyeballed during the build.

Before writing this post, I went back and measured. The real number is 98.57%.

It got worse: in 1,679 stratified job listings, the classifier returned confirmed exactly zero times.

This is the story of what that measurement revealed about the build I'd just shipped, the rules I'd carefully written that turned out to be dead code at runtime, and why that's the best thing that could have happened to the product.

The setup

Germany's Bundesagentur für Arbeit job board is the federal source — public API, ~1.6 million live listings, every relevant employer represented. It has every field a relocating engineer might want, except whether an employer will sponsor a work visa. There is no filter, no checkbox, no field.

So I wrote a classifier in TypeScript. Four rule sets, 46 rules total:

  • 20 hard-positive phrases in English and German: visa sponsorship, Blue Card, Blaue Karte EU, Skilled Worker visa, Fachkräfteeinwanderungsgesetz, relocation support, §18a/§18b AufenthG, etc.
  • 12 hard-negative phrases: no visa sponsorship, EU citizenship required, deutsche Staatsangehörigkeit erforderlich, security clearance required, SÜG security check, etc.
  • 10 soft positives: international team, working language English, D&I statement, global organization, remote / mobile work, etc.
  • 4 negative employer patterns for citizenship-restricted firms: Bundeswehr, BWI GmbH (Bundeswehr IT), federal agencies (BKA/BND/Bundespolizei), Verfassungsschutz.

The label logic:

  • confirmed = ≥2 hard-positives AND zero hard-negatives.
  • likely = exactly 1 hard-positive, no hard-negatives. Or ≥3 soft signals when no hard signal fires.
  • unlikely = any hard-negative AND no hard-positives.
  • unknown = everything else.

I wrote 13 unit tests covering the branches. They passed. Ship it.

The benchmark

To write this post honestly, I needed real numbers. So I ran the classifier on 1,679 listings stratified across 5 search terms:

  • Software Engineer — 400 (English-heavy tech)
  • Data Scientist — 282 (English-heavy tech)
  • Maschinenbauingenieur — 197 (German-heavy engineering)
  • Pflegefachkraft (nursing) — 400 (German-only nursing)
  • Marketing Manager — 400 (mixed business)

(Data Scientist and Maschinenbauingenieur returned fewer than 400 because the federal API exhausted at those totals. The sample spans English-heavy tech, German-heavy engineering, German-only nursing, and a mid-skill management category — if the classifier behaves differently across them, this dataset shows it.)

Results:

  • confirmed: 0 rows (0.00%)
  • likely: 16 rows (0.95%)
  • unlikely: 8 rows (0.48%)
  • unknown: 1,655 rows (98.57%)

Zero confirmed. Sixteen likely. Eight unlikely. One thousand, six hundred and fifty-five unknown.

The README said 93%. The truth is 98.57%. I shipped a product whose silent middle is even bigger than I claimed.

Where the few signals are

It gets sharper when you split by search term:

  • Software Engineer: 0 confirmed · 11 likely · 6 unlikely · 383 unknown
  • Data Scientist: 0 confirmed · 5 likely · 2 unlikely · 275 unknown
  • Maschinenbauingenieur: 0 confirmed · 0 likely · 0 unlikely · 197 unknown
  • Pflegefachkraft: 0 confirmed · 0 likely · 0 unlikely · 400 unknown
  • Marketing Manager: 0 confirmed · 0 likely · 0 unlikely · 400 unknown

Software Engineer and Data Scientist carry every signal in the dataset, on both sides. Mechanical engineering, nursing, and marketing return 100% unknown each. Zero employers in 997 job postings across those three categories used a single visa-related phrase the classifier could lock onto.

This isn't a bug in the rules. It's a signal about the market. German employers in nursing and traditional engineering simply do not mention visa policy in their job posts. They may sponsor or may not, but the description does not carry the evidence.

For an AI agent or relocation consultant filtering only confirmed + likely, the dataset shrinks from 1,679 to 16 rows. For a Pflegefachkraft candidate filtering that way: zero. The signal lives in unknown, or it doesn't live anywhere.

What the 46 rules actually do

Here's the part I didn't expect, and the part that should change how anyone designs this kind of classifier.

Of 20 hard-positive rules, exactly 5 ever fired:

  • relocation support — 6 hits
  • Skilled Worker visa — 2 hits
  • Umzugshilfe — 1 hit
  • visa sponsorship — 1 hit
  • work-permit support — 1 hit

The other 15 — including every German legal reference I was proudest of (§18a/§18b AufenthG, Aufenthaltstitel nach §18, Fachkräfteeinwanderungsgesetz, Blaue Karte EU, Wir helfen beim Visum) — fired zero times. They are dead code at runtime. I spent the better part of a day reading the Aufenthaltsgesetz to write correct German legal references into the rule list, and not one of them caught a real job.

Of 12 hard-negative rules, only 2 fired:

  • BWI GmbH (Bundeswehr IT) — 7 hits
  • SÜG security check (citizens only) — 1 hit

Of 4 negative-employer patterns, only BWI matched anything. Bundeswehr proper, Bundespolizei/BKA/BND, Verfassungsschutz: zero hits in 1,679 rows. They post jobs through different channels — or under names my naive employer-string matching missed. I'd rated this rule set as part of the moat. In practice it's one employer plus three placeholders.

The soft signals reveal a different problem. Seven of the ten soft rules fired at least once, so on paper they look healthy. The breakdown:

  • remote / mobile work — 409 hits (24.36% of all rows)
  • international team/environment — 58 hits (3.45%)
  • worldwide presence — 25 hits (1.49%)
  • D&I statement — 17 hits (1.01%)
  • global organization — 10 hits (0.60%)
  • Vielfalt & Inklusion — 6 hits (0.36%)
  • expat mentioned — 2 hits (0.12%)

The problem is concentrated in one row: remote / mobile work fires on 24% of all listings. That's not a visa signal — that's a post-pandemic baseline expectation that every fourth German job advertises. Yet it has equal weight in the ≥3-soft promotion rule alongside working language English and expat mentioned, which actually correlate with international hiring.

Of 5 rows that got soft-promoted from unknown to likely, every single one matched remote / mobile work plus two other generic globalization markers. Looking at them by hand: none read as obvious visa-friendly employers. NT-ware Systemprogrammierung is a print-management software company in Bad Iburg. CISPA is a Helmholtz research center — they probably do sponsor, but for an entirely different reason (academic visa track) than the soft signals my rules caught.

The tally: of 46 rules, 14 fired at least once. Of those 14, one fires far too often to mean what I want it to mean. The actual moat is 13 rules — not 46.

What this measurement changes

A normal post-mortem would file 30 issues to add more rules. That instinct is wrong, and I want to be specific about why.

### 1. The confirmed tier is empty in practice. Stop pricing for it.

Zero confirmed rows in 1,679 means German employers structurally do not write job descriptions that contain two explicit visa-sponsorship phrases. The bucket exists in code, but production never reaches it. Every UX decision that treats confirmed as the headline tier — pricing page tooltips, README examples, demo screenshots — is misleading.

The honest reframe: likely is the practical "yes" tier. The next version is likely to rename the buckets and drop confirmed from the headline framing in the pricing copy — there's no point selling a tier production doesn't reach.

### 2. The default filter must include unknown. This is load-bearing, not optional.

When I shipped, the input schema defaulted visaStatusFilter to ["confirmed", "likely", "unknown"]. I half-defended this as a thoughtful default. The benchmark proves it was the only honest default: excluding unknown shrinks the result set by 98.57%. A relocation consultant who flips the filter to ["confirmed", "likely"] to "see only verified roles" is getting 1.43% of the actual visa-sponsoring jobs in the market, because most visa-sponsoring employers simply don't mention sponsorship in the description.

The next version is likely to rename the field so this trade-off is impossible to misunderstand — probably something like dropSilentRows: false (default) versus dropSilentRows: true (explicit "I accept losing 99% of the dataset to read only loud signals").

### 3. Rule pruning beats rule adding.

I'm not going to write more rules. I'm going to remove remote / mobile work from the soft-signal list, downweight the broad-globalization markers, and concentrate the next iteration on the few rules that actually fire — making them more precise (negative lookbehinds for "we don't sponsor", "without relocation") rather than chasing breadth.

A classifier with 13 rules that all fire and all mean something will outperform a classifier with 46 rules where 32 are dead code. This is the regex-classifier equivalent of feature pruning in a sparse model. The moat is the precision of the rules that fire, not the count of rules you can list.

### 4. The LLM tie-breaker stops being optional. It's the actual classifier for the unknown tier.

The actor ships with a Claude Haiku tie-breaker as an opt-in escape hatch, capped at a configurable fraction of the run, priced at $0.001 per call. I positioned it as a nice-to-have.

Looking at 98.57% unknown, the tie-breaker isn't a nice-to-have — it's the only signal a buyer can get for almost every row in their result set. The next version probably needs to document this honestly: regex for the 1.43% of loud cases, LLM for everything else, with the buyer choosing whether the cost ceiling is worth the recall.

This is uncomfortable to write because it weakens my "deterministic and free!" marketing. It also happens to be true.

### 5. Build the benchmark into the actor.

The most important thing I did this week was run the benchmark. The next iteration should ideally wire it into the actor itself: every release publishing per-rule fire-rate numbers in the README, so the next version of me cannot ship a classifier with 32 dead rules without noticing.

This is the bigger lesson for anyone building data products on top of noisy real-world text: the rules you write feel like the product, but the rules that fire are the product. They are not always the same set. Measuring closes the gap.

What I'd tell a past me

Six days ago I would have called this classifier "tight" and "comprehensive." It is neither. It is sparse, two-thirds aspirational, and the one-third that works is leaning on a single soft signal that fires on every fourth job for reasons unrelated to visa policy.

The good news: the parts that don't work are visible, the parts that do work are honest, and the cost of fixing both is bounded by a half-week of focused rule pruning plus a real benchmark in CI. The next iteration should ship with fewer rules and more dignity.

The lesson I am taking into the next actor I build: write the benchmark before you write the rules. I had unit tests for the classifier from day one. I had aggregate fire-rate numbers from never. Unit tests prove a rule works on a contrived example. Benchmarks prove rules work together, on data that doesn't know you're watching. They are not the same thing, and only one of them ships.

The actor is here; the next iteration will likely prune the dead rules, surface the benchmark report in the README, and rename the schema so confirmed stops pretending to be a reachable state. If you're building a similar text-classifier-as-a-product, I'd love to hear what your fire-rate numbers look like once you measure.
